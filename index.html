Inside Boston’s Building Emissions Data

In the race to net zero, hospitals lag behind.

by By Anastasiia Boltkova, Hayes Botnick, Quyen Nguyen, Ying Niu

7 hours ago

On a weekday morning in Boston’s Longwood Medical Area, patients hurry between hospital buildings for appointments. Parents push strollers toward Boston Children’s Hospital. Doctors and nurses move quickly through the entrances as ambulances arrive outside the emergency department.

The volunteers hand out bags of food, toiletries, and other small necessities as those individuals set out to wander the streets for the rest of the night. Holyoke’s only homeless shelter – totaling 46 beds – has been full for months, and without funding for additional shelter beds this winter, there are few places for the unhoused to go. Even in nearby cities, beds aren’t available.

Just beyond the hospital towers, another structure rises above the neighborhood. The Medical Area Total Energy Plant supplies electricity, steam, and chilled water to many of the hospitals and research institutions in Longwood.

The plant rarely appears in conversations about public health. Yet, it powers the buildings where doctors treat respiratory illness, conduct medical research, and care for thousands of patients every day.

Hospitals run around the clock and must maintain tightly controlled ventilation, temperature, and safety systems to protect patients and staff. Operating rooms, laboratories, and intensive care units rely on constant airflow, specialized medical equipment, and strict climate controls required by health regulations. Those systems often run continuously, leaving little room to reduce energy use compared with offices, schools, or residential buildings.

This issue matters as Boston works to reduce climate pollution from buildings, which remain the city’s largest source of greenhouse gas emissions.

Hospitals, emissions, and Boston’s climate goals

City data shows that buildings account for about 70% of Boston’s total greenhouse gas emissions.

“About 70% of the city’s emissions come from buildings,” said Hassan Farooqi, executive director of the Boston Climate Action Network, citing data from the City of Boston. “If the city wants to make real progress on climate goals, the building sector is where the biggest changes have to happen.”

To address that challenge, Boston created the Building Emissions Reduction and Disclosure Ordinance, known as BERDO. This policy requires large buildings to report their energy use and gradually reduce emissions in order to reach net-zero emissions by 2050. The ordinance applies to commercial buildings larger than 20,000 square feet and residential buildings with more than 15 units.

BERDO’s main metric is the Energy Star score: a scoring system that measures energy performance against similar building types. Each year, building owners are required to enter their energy data and building specifications for an accurate rating.

The dataset also makes it possible to compare how different types of buildings perform across Boston. Those comparisons reveal clear differences between building types and hospitals that stand out in the data.

Hospitals’ High Energy Demand

Part of that gap comes down to how differently hospitals use energy. Unlike most buildings, hospitals operate around the clock and must maintain tightly controlled environments for patient safety. Ventilation systems run continuously, temperatures must remain stable, and specialized medical equipment requires constant power.

At Boston University, sustainability data analyst Gabrielle Brewer describes hospital energy demand in simple terms: “Hospitals and laboratories require much more energy and operate 24 hours a day.”

At Boston Children’s Hospital, energy systems manager Brian Smith points to ventilation as one of the biggest drivers. In clinical spaces, he explained, systems may require “20 to 40 air changes an hour at 50 or 60 degrees, when it’s 100 degrees outside.”

Those requirements help protect patients from infection, but they also push hospital energy use far beyond most other building types. Even so, hospital operations alone do not fully explain the emissions picture in Boston’s Longwood Medical Area.

The Energy Plant Behind Longwood’s Hospitals

In Boston’s Longwood Medical Area, several major hospitals rely on the Medical Area Total Energy Plant (MATEP), a centralized facility that supplies electricity, steam, and chilled water to the entire medical cluster. For operating rooms, laboratories, and intensive care units, where even a brief power interruption could endanger patients, that level of energy reliability is essential.

The plant operates on a cogeneration model, producing electricity and heat from the same fuel source. Two natural gas turbines and six diesel engines generate power on site. Instead of venting hot exhaust as waste, the facility captures that heat to produce steam for nearby hospitals and research buildings, allowing the system to extract more energy from each unit of fuel than a conventional power plant. Plant operators emphasize reliability as the system’s core mission.

But that efficiency carries environmental costs for surrounding neighborhoods. Each year, the plant releases roughly 290,000 tons of carbon dioxide, nearly 600 tons of nitrogen oxides, and significant amounts of particulate pollution. The facility sits next to Mission Hill, Roxbury, and Jamaica Plain — neighborhoods where residents have raised concerns about air quality and respiratory illness since the plant was first proposed in the 1970s.

Pressure to reduce emissions from the facility has grown in recent years. A coalition of advocacy groups, including the Massachusetts Clean Peak Coalition, Climate Code Blue, SEAM, and YouthCAN has called for the plant’s older turbines to be replaced with renewable energy and battery storage, arguing that cleaner alternatives are technically feasible even in a dense urban setting.

In February 2025, Regina LaRocque, an associate professor of medicine at Harvard Medical School, co-authored a public commentary criticizing the university's continued reliance on the plant, pointing to what she described as a gap between Harvard's public climate commitments and the fossil-fuel infrastructure operating in the Longwood area.

For Farooqi, the problem is difficult to solve without addressing the energy system itself: “For any of these hospitals to meet the city’s net-zero targets, they will have to address emissions tied to the Longwood energy system.”

Hospitals in the Longwood area are tied to the facility through long-term utility agreements that run through 2051. Those contracts were signed when the current operator, ENGIE North America, took over the plant in 2018.

That arrangement effectively ties the Longwood medical campus to fossil-fuel-dependent energy for decades to come, even as many of its institutions publicly commit to ambitious climate goals.

Newer buildings point to a different future

At Boston Children’s Hospital, leaders say newer facilities are already demonstrating how modern design can significantly reduce energy use. A key example is the Hale Family Clinical Building, a 600,000-square-foot clinical tower that opened in 2022.

New hospital construction is beginning to show what lower-emission healthcare buildings could look like.

“The energy use intensity of that building is less than half of the previous clinical building that we probably built maybe 20 years ago,” said Brian Smith.

Energy use intensity (EUI), is a common way to measure how much energy a building uses compared to its size. It looks at the total energy a building consumes in a year and divides it by the number of square feet in the building. In general, the lower the EUI, the more efficiently the building is operating and the fewer emissions it produces.

Hospital officials say newer buildings tend to perform better because they are designed using more modern efficiency standards. Many of today’s facilities include better insulation, thicker and more energy-efficient windows, and updated building systems that use less energy to heat, cool, and power the building.

“It’s just easier to run a space efficiently when it is well insulated, when you have thicker windows,” said Kate Lewandowski, director of sustainability at Boston Children’s Hospital.

Building new facilities lets hospitals design for energy efficiency from the beginning. Lewandowski said the hospital’s engineering and facilities teams consider energy use early in planning, making it a key part of how new buildings and future campus projects are built.

A similar approach can be seen in academic buildings. At Boston University, the Duan Family Center for Computing & Data Sciences (CDS) demonstrates how emissions can be minimized at every level. The building has no gas lines or fossil-fuel systems, and even small details, from paint and sealants to base elevation and shading angles, were chosen with sustainability in mind. “CDS is the largest fully fossil-fuel-free and net-zero building in the city of Boston,” said Sam Moller of BU Sustainability. Because it is fully electric and powered by renewable energy, the building met emissions goals from day one, and compliance is never a concern, according to Gabrielle Brewer of BU Sustainability.

But that approach works best when creating something new. The CDS building didn’t replace an old building; it created an entirely new faculty of Computing and Data Science. While few institutions could implement a project like this without significant funding, it shows the potential of designing buildings with sustainability embedded from the ground up.

Whether it’s a clinical tower at Boston Children’s or an academic tower at BU, these examples highlight what’s possible when sustainability is built in from the start. Older hospitals and public schools face bigger challenges, but design-first approaches can offer long-lasting, energy-efficient solutions.

Progress, Pressure, and the Path to 2050

“Different building types have different constraints. There’s no one-size-fits-all solution. What works for a university building may not work for a K–8 school or a hospital,” said Mahler.

Boston’s building data suggests that reaching the city’s climate goals will require different strategies for different buildings.

New projects such as Boston University’s Center for Computing and Data Sciences show what is possible when sustainability is built into a building from the start. The tower runs without fossil fuels and uses renewable electricity, allowing it to meet the city’s emissions targets from day one.

But most buildings in Boston were constructed decades ago. Hospitals operate around the clock and rely on energy-intensive ventilation and medical systems to protect patients. Many are also tied to shared energy infrastructure that limits how quickly they can reduce emissions. Public schools face another set of challenges: aging facilities and limited budgets make major upgrades difficult, even when the technical solutions are clear.

The technology to cut emissions already exists. The harder question is how quickly those solutions can be applied to the buildings people rely on every day.

As Boston moves toward its 2050 net-zero target, the path forward may depend less on discovering new technologies than on whether the city and the institutions that power it can adapt existing ones in time.
